About [User's Name]

I started tinkering with circuits and motors in my parents' garage, breaking things to understand how they worked. That curiosity turned into a career solving real-world engineering problems—from optimizing manufacturing workflows to designing custom automation systems. I love the moment when a prototype finally works, when theory meets reality and the solution is both elegant and practical.

For me, engineering isn't just about making things function; it's about making them repeatable, documented, and validated. I collaborate best when there's open communication and room for iteration. Whether it's a mechanical assembly, an electrical control system, or a software integration, I believe in shipping solutions that others can understand, maintain, and build upon.

"Done" means tested under real conditions, documented with clear schematics or diagrams, and validated against the original requirements. It means the next engineer (or my future self) can pick it up without guessing.

Working style

I prefer short iteration cycles with regular check-ins. I'll share progress early and often—sketches, breadboards, rough prototypes—so we can course-correct before investing too much time. Clear communication and honest feedback keep projects on track and make collaboration productive.

Principles

Make constraints visible

Engineering portfolios thrive when they expose the real limits—material, budget, time—that shaped each decision. Documenting constraints turns a simple project showcase into a story of thoughtful trade-offs and creative problem-solving.

Validate early

Test assumptions before they calcify into architecture. Quick prototypes, breadboard circuits, and rough CAD sketches catch misalignments faster than polished deliverables ever will.

Design for the next builder

Every schematic, every line of code, every material choice should assume someone else will pick up where you left off. Clear documentation and modular design aren't luxuries—they're respect for future collaborators.
